\begin{abstract}
We present an open, reproducible deep learning framework for short-horizon (96
hour) consumption-based carbon intensity forecasting that explicitly models
cross-border electricity flows, evaluated across five structurally diverse grid
zones: US-MIDA-PJM, US-NY-NYIS, Finland, Belgium, and Singapore. The framework
reproduces the closed CarbonCast methodology as a production-based baseline,
extends it to a consumption-based target with interconnector flow and partner
carbon intensity inputs, and compares both against a single-tier model that
forecasts carbon intensity directly. We contribute a per-region accuracy atlas, a
structural ablation of the two-tier design, a feature ablation of the cross-border
inputs, and a characterization of where forecasts succeed and fail.

\section{Introduction and Problem}
Data center electricity demand is rising sharply, and the carbon emissions a workload causes
depends on when and where it runs. The operationally correct signal for a
carbon-aware scheduler is \emph{consumption-based} carbon intensity, which
accounts for the carbon embedded in imported electricity, rather than the
\emph{production-based} intensity of locally generated power that most academic
forecasters target. This report develops a short-horizon (96 hour) forecasts of
consumption-based carbon intensity, opens a closed methodology, and characterizes
performance across heterogeneous grids. The contribution is not introducing
consumption-based forecasting as a target, since operational systems already
exist, but opening a closed methodology, ablating the role of energy interconnector
flows, characterizing where forecasts succeed and fail across heterogeneous grid
compositions, and providing a reproducible benchmark.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{CarbonCast} (Maji, Shenoy, Sitaraman, ACM BuildSys 2022) is the closest
methodological anchor. It is a two-tier hierarchical model: Tier~1 is a set of
per-source feedforward networks that forecast generation from history, calendar
features, and weather; Tier~2 is a convolutional-recurrent network that consumes
the Tier~1 forecasts together with historical carbon intensity and weather to
forecast carbon intensity. It is production-based by design, explicitly ignoring
imports and exports, and is evaluated on six regions (one, PJM, overlaps with our
set). It is open source. We reproduce it as our production-based baseline.

\textbf{EnsembleCI} (Yan et al., 2025) is an adaptive ensemble that improves on
CarbonCast for production-based carbon intensity across eleven grids including PJM,
but does not use interconnector flows. It is the strongest production-based prior
art.

\textbf{CarbonFlex} (Hanafy et al., 2025) and the \textbf{Phoenix field
demonstration} (Colangelo et al., 2025) are not forecasting papers; they are
downstream consumers of carbon intensity forecasts. We cite them to motivate the
carbon-aware computing application, not as baselines.

\textbf{Electricity Maps operational forecast} (productionized January 2025)
provides flow-traced, consumption-based carbon intensity forecasts across more
than one hundred zones and is used by Google's carbon-intelligent computing
platform. It is a closed model and is the operational benchmark our
consumption-based work is positioned against.

\textbf{The gap.} None of these does open consumption-based forecasting with
explicit cross-border flow modeling for comparable regions. EnsembleCI is closest
but production-based; the Electricity Maps forecast is consumption-based but
closed. This work sits in the gap between open and production-based and closed and
consumption-based.

\section{Data}
We use Electricity Maps (academic license) for hourly carbon intensity, power
breakdown, and electricity flows, and Open-Meteo (ERA5 reanalysis for history,
GFS for forecasts) for weather, over January 2021 through May 2026 across the five
zones. Carbon intensity is computed with CarbonCast Table~1 lifecycle emission
factors. For each zone we also collect the carbon intensity history of its
interconnector partners (twenty external zones), used as a consumption-based
feature.

The five zones were chosen for structural diversity: a large multi-state US market
(PJM), a smaller US market with heavy hydro imports (NYIS), a Nordic grid
undergoing a nuclear-driven decarbonization (Finland), a small import-heavy
European grid (Belgium), and a near-islanded gas-dominated grid (Singapore).

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Two-tier architecture}
Following CarbonCast, the two-tier model couples per-source Tier~1 feedforward
networks that forecast generation with a Tier~2 convolutional-recurrent network
that consumes those forecasts together with historical carbon intensity, weather,
and calendar features to emit a 96 hour carbon intensity vector. We extend Tier~1
with per-interconnector flow networks that forecast signed net flow, and we add
partner carbon intensity as a Tier~2 channel. To avoid in-sample leakage, the
Tier~1 forecasts used for Tier~2 training are generated out of fold (leave one
year out); final Tier~1 models drive inference.

\subsection{Production and consumption targets}
The production-based variant (the faithful baseline) targets the domestic
generation-mix intensity. The consumption-based variant targets the
Electricity Maps reported consumption intensity and adds the cross-border channels.

\subsection{Single-tier baseline}
To test whether the two-tier structure earns its complexity, we compare against a
single-tier model: the same Tier~2 network run standalone, with no Tier~1 forecast
channels, predicting carbon intensity directly from its own history plus weather
and calendar. Because the network is identical, the comparison isolates the
contribution of the two-tier design.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\textbf{Splits.} Training 2021 through 2025, validation January through April 2026,
Test~A May 2026, and a live Test~B (June 8 to 21, 2026) head-to-head against the
Electricity Maps operational forecast. Finland uses a shortened training start of
2023 (a per-zone window), for the regime-shift reason in Section~4.1; the other
zones use the full window. Each zone has its own model, so a per-zone window costs
nothing for the stable zones.

\textbf{Training.} RMSE loss, Adam with gradient clipping. Each model is trained on
three random seeds and the model with the best validation score is kept
(Section~\ref{sec:findings}).

\section{Iterative Findings and Modeling Decisions}
\label{sec:findings}
Our methodology was shaped by a sequence of empirical findings, recorded here
because they justify the final design.

\textbf{Initialization sensitivity.} Early five-zone runs showed large
run-to-run variation driven purely by the random initial weights: on the harder
zones some seeds converged well while others diverged, to a non-finite loss or a
poor solution. On Belgium the seed-to-seed spread in percentage error exceeded the
size of any plausible modeling improvement.

\textbf{Seeding and validation-based selection.} We fixed the random seed for
reproducibility and adopted a selection rule: train several seeds and keep the one
with the best \emph{validation} score, never the test score. This removes the seed
lottery, because a divergent seed also scores poorly on validation.

\textbf{Gradient clipping.} The non-finite-loss failures were exploding gradients;
adding gradient clipping removed them, with stable seeds unaffected.

\textbf{Belgium as an outlier.} Belgium showed both the highest error and the
largest instability. Even after stabilization its best-validation model generalized
poorly from the validation months to Test~A (validation near 33, Test~A near 64),
indicating a genuine distribution shift in May 2026 rather than a training
artifact. We report this as a failure mode rather than correcting it away.

\textbf{Finland regime shift and shortened training.} Inspecting carbon intensity
over time revealed the Finnish nuclear-driven regime shift (Section~4.1). We
shortened Finland's training window to begin in 2023 and re-ran the models.

\begin{abstract}
We present an open, reproducible deep learning framework for short-horizon (96
hour) consumption-based carbon intensity forecasting that explicitly models
cross-border electricity flows, evaluated across five structurally diverse energy grid
zones: US-MIDA-PJM (US Mid-Atlantic grid), US-NY-NYIS (New York grid), Finland, Belgium, and Singapore. The framework
reproduces the closed CarbonCast method as a production-based baseline,
extends it to a consumption-based target with interconnector flow and partner
carbon intensity inputs, and compares both against a single-tier model that
forecasts carbon intensity directly. We contribute a per-region accuracy atlas, a
structural comparison of the two-tier design, and a characterization of where
forecasts succeed and fail. On a live June 2026 window, with models replayed on actual rather than forecast
weather (an optimistic upper bound, see Section~\ref{sec:testb}), the open model
is within two MAPE points of the operational Electricity Maps forecast in three of
five zones with larger gaps in the two transitioning grids. Within the two-tier consumption
framework, turning the cross-border flow inputs off shows they do not improve
accuracy: the model is as good or better without them in four of five zones.

On a held-out May 2026 test month, consumption-based accuracy ranges from under 1\% MAPE in near-islanded Singapore to roughly 6\% in the large US markets, with Belgium the hard case, a distribution shift that training on recent data only partly closes. Our central finding is that the two-tier structure does not earn its complexity: a single-tier network that forecasts carbon intensity directly matches or beats the two-tier
model in every zone, because imperfect first-tier generation forecasts inject more
noise than signal. On PJM the production-based two-tier model is broadly consistent
with the published CarbonCast result (6.0 versus 5.3 MAPE).
\end{abstract}

% ── §1. Introduction and Problem ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction and Problem}
In recent years, global electricity demand has risen sharply, growing by more than
4\% in 2024 and projected to keep climbing through the rest of the decade~\cite{iea2024electricity}.
Data centers are among the fastest-growing sources of this load: their electricity
use is projected to more than double, from roughly 460 TWh in 2022 to over
1{,}000 TWh by 2026~\cite{iea2024electricity}. Meeting that demand is a challenge, but it also creates
an opening. Because a computing workload can run in different places and at
different times, and the emissions it causes depend on when and where it runs,
operators can move data center work toward cleaner grids and cleaner hours and cut
its carbon footprint. Acting on this requires an accurate, location-specific
measure of how carbon-intense a region's electricity is.

Most prior work predicts a region's carbon intensity from its own energy
production. That production-based view is incomplete, because a region's real
footprint also depends on the electricity it imports from its neighbors and on how
that imported power was generated. The signal a carbon-aware scheduler actually
needs is \emph{consumption-based} carbon intensity, which accounts for the carbon
embedded in imported electricity rather than only the \emph{production-based}
intensity of locally generated power that academic forecasting work has predominantly targeted~\cite{maji2022carboncast,yan2025ensembleci}.

This project extends previous carbon intensity forecasting frameworks to the
consumption-based target by modeling the net energy exchange between five zones and
their interconnector partners, producing a short-horizon (96 hour) forecast that
can inform systems scheduling data center workloads by a region's carbon intensity.
The contribution is not introducing consumption-based forecasting as a target,
since operational systems already exist, but opening a closed method, measuring
the role of energy interconnector flows (and finding that, at the studied horizon,
they do not improve accuracy), characterizing where forecasts succeed and fail across
diverse grids, and providing a reproducible benchmark.
Code and trained models are available at \url{https://github.com/nicolaswilches/carbon-intensity-forecast} under the MIT license.

% ── §2. Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\textbf{CarbonCast}~\cite{maji2022carboncast} is the closest
reference. It is a two-tier model: Tier~1 is a set of
per-source feedforward artificial neural networks (ANNs) that forecast generation from history, calendar features, and weather; Tier~2 is a convolutional neural network feeding a long short-term memory layer (CNN-LSTM) that consumes the Tier~1 forecasts together with historical carbon intensity and weather to forecast carbon intensity. It is production-based by design, explicitly ignoring imports and exports, and is evaluated on six regions, one of them PJM, which this study also covers. Because it is open source~\cite{ucscospre2025}, we adopt CarbonCast as the two-tier \emph{production-based} baseline that our two-tier \emph{consumption-based} model extends.

\textbf{EnsembleCI}~\cite{yan2025ensembleci} is an adaptive ensemble that improves on
CarbonCast for production-based carbon intensity across eleven grids including PJM,
but does not use interconnector flows. It is a strong production-based prior art,
reporting lower MAPE than CarbonCast on PJM.

\textbf{Electricity Maps operational forecast}~\cite{electricitymaps2025forecast} (productionized January 2025)
provides flow-traced, consumption-based carbon intensity forecasts across more
than one hundred zones and is used by Google's carbon-intelligent computing
platform~\cite{radovanovic2023carbon}. It is a closed model, and the operational forecast our consumption-based
work is benchmarked against.

\textbf{CarbonFlex}~\cite{hanafy2025carbonflex} and the \textbf{Phoenix field
demonstration}~\cite{colangelo2025phoenix} are not forecasting papers but
downstream consumers of carbon intensity forecasts, cited to motivate the
carbon-aware computing application rather than as baselines.

\textbf{The gap.} None of these offers open, consumption-based forecasting with
explicit cross-border flow modeling for comparable regions. EnsembleCI is the
strongest academic baseline but stays production-based, while the Electricity Maps forecast
is consumption-based but closed. This work fills the empty corner: an open,
reproducible model that targets consumption-based carbon intensity and models
cross-border flows explicitly.

% ── §3. Data ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data}

We use Electricity Maps~\cite{electricitymaps2025forecast} (academic license) for hourly carbon intensity, power breakdown, and electricity flows, and Open-Meteo~\cite{openmeteo} (ERA5 reanalysis~\cite{hersbach2020era5} for history,
GFS~\cite{noaagfs} for forecasts) for weather, over January 2021 through May 2026 across the five zones. We use two carbon-intensity signals. We compute the \emph{production-based} intensity, the quantity CarbonCast targets and the label for our production-based baseline, from the domestic generation mix exactly as CarbonCast does~\cite{maji2022carboncast}:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CI}^{\mathrm{prod}}_t = \frac{\sum_i E_{i,t}\,\mathrm{CEF}_i}{\sum_i E_{i,t}}
\end{equation}
\vspace{0.5em}

where $E_{i,t}$ is the generation of source $i$ in hour $t$ and $\mathrm{CEF}_i$ its
emission factor from CarbonCast Table~1, in gCO$_2$eq/kWh. All carbon intensity
values throughout this paper are in gCO$_2$eq/kWh. We take the lifecycle (scope~3) factors as the target. We treat
emission factors as fixed constants following CarbonCast; updating them to reflect
capacity-vintage changes over 2022--2026 is left for future work. This signal
ignores imports and exports by construction. The \emph{consumption-based} intensity,
our main target, is not recomputed: we use the flow-traced value Electricity Maps
reports, which already accounts for the carbon embedded in imported electricity. The
cross-border information enters the forecasting model as input features, the
per-partner carbon intensity and net interconnector flows introduced below, rather
than as an additional term in a carbon-intensity formula. For each zone we also
collect the carbon intensity history of its interconnector partners (twenty external
zones, the direct physical neighbors identified by Electricity Maps' interconnector
topology and derived automatically from the flow columns in the extracted data),
used as a consumption-based feature.

The five zones were chosen for structural diversity: a large multi-state US market
(PJM), a smaller US market with heavy hydro imports (NYIS), a Nordic grid
undergoing a nuclear-driven decarbonization (Finland), a small import-heavy
European grid (Belgium), and a near-islanded gas-dominated grid (Singapore).

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
This section reports our initial evaluation of the extracted data across all five
regions. We begin with the temporal evolution of carbon intensity in each region,
then turn to its distribution within each zone alongside the makeup of the generation
mix. Together these show how sharply each region's carbon intensity has shifted over
the five-year window, and how closely those shifts track the changing energy mix. We
next examine each region's energy trade with its partners, measured as total imports
and exports across all interconnectors. Finally, we look at how temporal patterns
shape carbon intensity and how the main features correlate with it.

% §4.3 Energy mix evolution per region
\subsection{Energy mix evolution per region}
Figure~\ref{fig:mix-evolution} shows per-source generation share from 2021 to 2025.
The five grids are not moving in the same direction or at the same speed, and in each
case the shift in the data lines up with a concrete policy or asset change we can name
and source.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1em]
\item \textbf{Belgium:} nuclear falls sharply, 52\% to 33\%, while solar nearly
triples (5\% to 15\%) and wind grows (12\% to 18\%); gas holds near 20\%. This reflects the country's nuclear phase-out trajectory: the 2003 federal law capped
reactor lifetimes, and although a 2022 government decision extended two reactors by
ten years, most capacity was already curtailed during our study window; the IEA warned
that retiring this firm, low-carbon capacity would raise the carbon intensity of Belgian
electricity~\cite{iea2022belgium}, which is exactly what the data show. Firm nuclear is giving way to weather-driven renewables that gas must back up, so Belgium's intensity both rises and swings with wind and sunlight. It is the largest structural change in the set and the root of Belgium's forecasting difficulty later in the paper.

\item \textbf{Finland:} wind more than doubles (12\% to 27\%) and nuclear edges up
(36\% to 39\%), while coal collapses (11\% to 2\%) and gas all but disappears (7\%
to 1\%); hydro eases from 23\% to 15\%. The nuclear step coincides with Olkiluoto~3, a
1{,}600~MW reactor that began regular production in April 2023 and now supplies close
to a third of Finnish electricity~\cite{tvo2023ol3}. Finland is
decarbonizing on two fronts at once, adding a block of new nuclear and a wave of
wind while retiring fossils, and the combination halves its carbon intensity
over the five-year window, the steepest decline in the set.

\item \textbf{Singapore:} gas dominates throughout (97\% to 95\%) with no nuclear,
and only solar moves, from a thin 0.7\% to 2.8\%. This matches the national fuel mix
the Energy Market Authority reports, where natural gas supplies roughly 94\% of
generation and renewables are held back by limited land and the grid's near-islanded
position~\cite{emases}. With almost no interconnection and a single dominant fuel, the
mix has little room to change, so intensity stays flat and high, which is also why
Singapore is by far the easiest zone to forecast.

\item \textbf{US-MIDA-PJM:} a slow coal-to-gas shift on a stable base: gas rises
38\% to 42\% and coal falls 22\% to 17\%, with nuclear steady near 32\% and solar
still small (0.7\% to 2.8\%). This is the long-running PJM transition the EIA
documents, in which cheap Appalachian gas and efficient combined-cycle plants have
steadily displaced retiring coal while a large nuclear fleet holds the low-carbon
base~\cite{eia2018pjmgas}. Because the change is gradual and that nuclear block anchors
the mix, intensity barely moves across the window; the size and diversity of the
market give it strong inertia, the kind of well-behaved signal that is comparatively
easy to model.

\item \textbf{US-NY-NYIS:} gas grows (48\% to 53\%) while both low-carbon sources
recede, nuclear 25\% to 21\% and hydro 22\% to 18\%, with wind only 3\% to 5\%. The
nuclear drop lines up with the 2021 retirement of Indian Point, whose roughly 2~GW of
low-carbon output was largely replaced by fossil gas generation~\cite{eia2021indianpoint}. Here the trend runs the wrong way: as nuclear
capacity leaves and hydro's share slips, gas fills the gap and intensity climbs, with
renewables not yet scaling fast enough to offset the lost low-carbon generation.
\end{itemize}

Taken together the five grids span most of the range a forecaster can face: one

\subsection{Feature relationships and correlations}

The correlations are consistent and interpretable across all five zones. Production intensity is the dominant
predictor everywhere (0.86 to 1.00). Partner carbon intensity is strong (0.71 to

% ── §5. Methodology ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

% §5.1 Two-tier architecture
\subsection{Two-tier architecture}
Following CarbonCast, the two-tier model couples per-source Tier~1 feedforward
networks that forecast generation with a Tier~2 CNN-LSTM
that consumes those forecasts together with historical carbon intensity, weather,
and calendar features to emit a 96-hour carbon intensity vector. Unlike the
original CarbonCast, which iterates four 24-hour windows to reach 96 hours, the
Tier~2 head predicts all 96 steps in a single forward pass. Iterating would
propagate Tier~1 forecast error through each chaining step, compounding across
the horizon; a direct multi-step head avoids this accumulation~\cite{taieb2016bias}.
We extend Tier~1 with per-interconnector flow networks that forecast signed net
flow, and we add partner carbon intensity as a Tier~2 channel.

Tier~2 always consumes Tier~1 \emph{forecasts}, never actual future generation, so
no future information leaks into the carbon-intensity prediction. The forecasts used
to train Tier~2 are generated out of fold, with leave-one-year-out Tier~1 models
predicting each training year from the others. This matters because in-sample
Tier~1 forecasts, produced by models that have already seen those hours, are
unrealistically accurate, and training Tier~2 on them would teach it to over-trust
the first tier and overstate its own accuracy. Out-of-fold forecasts instead carry
the same error profile Tier~2 meets at inference, where the chain is always the
final Tier~1 models followed by Tier~2.

% §5.2 Production and consumption targets
\subsection{Production and consumption targets}
The production-based variant (the faithful baseline) targets the domestic
generation-mix intensity. The consumption-based variant targets the
Electricity Maps reported consumption intensity and adds the cross-border channels.

% §5.3 Single-tier baseline
\subsection{Single-tier baseline}
To test whether the two-tier structure earns its complexity, we compare against a
single-tier model: the same Tier~2 network run standalone, with no Tier~1 forecast
channels, predicting carbon intensity directly from its own history plus weather
and calendar. Because the network is identical, the comparison isolates the
contribution of the two-tier design.

% §5.4 Implementation details
\subsection{Implementation details}
Tier~2 uses Conv1D(4, kernel\,4) $\to$ MaxPooling1D(2) $\to$ Conv1D(16, kernel\,4) $\to$ LSTM(24) $\to$ Dropout(0.1) $\to$ Dense(96). Tier~1 source and flow ANNs use a Dense(50 $\to$ 34 $\to$ 24 $\to$ 96) stack with ReLU activations. All networks use RMSE loss, Adam (learning rate 0.001, gradient clipping norm 1.0), batch size 32, and early stopping on validation loss with patience 10 over a maximum of 100 epochs. The Tier~2 input sequence spans 264 timesteps (168-hour lookback plus 96-hour forecast horizon). Tier~1 networks are trained independently per source and per interconnector.

% ── §6. Experimental Setup ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Experimental Setup}
\textbf{Splits.} Training 2021 through 2025, validation January through April 2026,
Test~A May 2026, and a live Test~B (June 8 to 21, 2026) head-to-head against the
Electricity Maps operational forecast. Both transitioning grids (Finland and Belgium) use a 2024-onward training window,
motivated by the regime shifts documented in Section~4.1 and confirmed on the
validation set (Section~\ref{sec:findings}). The three stable grids use the full
2021-2025 window. A single cutoff applies to both transitioning zones, so no
per-zone tuning is required.

\textbf{Why two test sets.} The two windows answer different questions, and neither
alone is enough. Test~A is a complete held-out month drawn from the historical
data, scored at the full 1 to 96 hour horizon against the realized carbon
intensity. Because every framework runs through the same offline pipeline on it,
Test~A carries the internal comparisons: the per-region accuracy atlas, the
single-tier versus two-tier structural result, and the CarbonCast reproduction. What
it cannot do is benchmark against the operational baseline, because the Electricity
Maps forecast is published live and not archived, so it cannot be reconstructed for
an arbitrary past month. Test~B fills that gap. Over June 8 to 21, 2026 we captured
the Electricity Maps forecast as it was issued, every six hours, alongside the weather
forecast the model would have seen at that moment. This is a non-recoverable clock:
the operational forecast for a given hour exists only until it is overwritten, so it
had to be collected in real time rather than pulled afterward. Test~B therefore makes
two things possible that Test~A cannot: a head-to-head against the closed operational
forecast on identical inputs, and a second, fully out-of-sample window that checks
whether the May structural finding still holds in June. Its horizon is capped at 1 to
24 hours by the academic license (Section~\ref{sec:other-limits}), so the two windows
are reported separately rather than pooled.

\textbf{Training.} RMSE loss, Adam with gradient clipping. Each model is trained on
three random seeds and the model with the best validation score is kept
(Section~\ref{sec:findings}).


% ── §7. Iterative Findings and Modeling Decisions ────────────────────────────
\section{Iterative Findings and Modeling Decisions}
\label{sec:findings}
Our approach was shaped by a sequence of findings from experiments, recorded here
because they justify the final design.

\textbf{Initialization sensitivity.} Early five-zone runs showed large
run-to-run variation driven purely by the random initial weights: on the harder
zones some seeds converged well while others diverged, to a non-finite loss or a
poor solution. On Belgium the seed-to-seed spread in percentage error exceeded the
size of any plausible modeling improvement.

\textbf{Seeding and validation-based selection.} We fixed the random seed for
reproducibility and adopted a selection rule: train three seeds and keep the one
with the best \emph{validation} score, never the test score. This removes the seed
lottery, because a divergent seed also scores poorly on validation.

\textbf{Gradient clipping.} The non-finite-loss failures were exploding gradients;
adding gradient clipping removed them, with stable seeds unaffected.

\textbf{Belgium as an outlier.} Belgium showed both the highest error and the
largest instability. Even after stabilization its best-validation model generalized
poorly from the validation months to Test~A, the validation error far below the
test error (Table~\ref{tab:atlas}), indicating a genuine distribution shift in May
2026 rather than a training artifact. The training-window experiment below shows
that restricting Belgium to recent data substantially reduces this gap, since the
pre-phase-out history no longer matches the test period.

\textbf{Training window for transitioning grids.} The EDA revealed structural
regime shifts in both Finland and Belgium (Section~4.1): Finland's domestic
nuclear capacity more than doubled after 2022, sharply lowering its carbon
intensity; Belgium's nuclear phase-out drove its capacity down over the same
period. In both cases, history before the shift describes a grid that no longer
exists at test time. We therefore restrict both transitioning grids to a 2024
onward training window, leaving the three stable grids (Singapore, PJM, NYIS) on
the full 2021-2025 history. The window is the same for both transitioning zones,
avoiding any per-zone tuning. Validation accuracy confirmed the direction: for
Belgium the shortened window lowered validation MAPE across all four frameworks;
for Finland the validation improvement was smaller but consistent, reflecting a
slower-moving shift that still benefits from excluding the pre-nuclear-ramp period.
The final design uses a single cutoff: 2024 onward for Finland and Belgium, full
history for the three stable grids.

% ── §8. Results ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
We evaluate four frameworks: a single-tier network and the two-tier
(CarbonCast-style) pipeline, each trained on the production-based and the

% §8.3 Error by forecast horizon
\subsection{Error by forecast horizon}
Figures~\ref{fig:horizon-mape} and~\ref{fig:horizon-mae} trace per-zone error, as
MAPE and MAE, across the full 1 to 96 hour
horizon. Error does not simply grow with lead time; for every zone the curve stays
roughly flat, so the day-ahead band is not markedly better than the four-day band.

direct 96-step head predicts all horizons in a single forward pass rather than by
chaining shorter windows, so forecast error cannot compound across steps. Second,
carbon intensity carries a strong autocorrelation that persists well beyond one day
(Section~4.6), giving the model a stable signal at the long end of the horizon.
Belgium sits highest throughout, near 30\%, with the other four well below. Because
the curves are nearly flat, the 1 to 24 hour band of the Electricity Maps
head-to-head (Section~\ref{sec:testb}) is no worse than the longer horizons.

% ── §9. Discussion and Limitations ───────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion and Limitations}

% §9.1 Tier 1 forecast quality and the two-tier structure
\subsection{Tier 1 forecast quality and the two-tier structure}
A single-tier model that ignores Tier~1 entirely matches or beats the two-tier
model. The causal chain is straightforward: the two-tier model passes Tier~1
generation forecasts as additional input channels to Tier~2, while the single-tier
model uses no generation channels at all. If those Tier~1 forecasts are inaccurate,
they inject noise rather than signal into Tier~2. The single-tier model, carrying
no such noisy inputs, is insulated from Tier~1 error. That it wins is therefore
consistent with, and predicted by, the Tier~1 error diagnostic that follows. Inspecting Tier~1 directly explains why. Table~\ref{tab:tier1} reports the
normalized RMSE (RMSE over the mean) of the total-generation forecast by horizon,
and Figures~\ref{fig:tier1-horizon} and~\ref{fig:tier1-pred} visualize it. The
takeaway is the same in every view: the Tier~1 generation forecasts are not accurate
enough to help. Their error propagates into Tier~2 as noise and caps what the
two-tier model can achieve, which is precisely why it never beats the single-tier
baseline. The two figures are the evidence for this limitation, one in aggregate and
one up close.

Two patterns emerge. Belgium has the worst Tier~1 error at every horizon, already
near 18\% one hour ahead and climbing past 25\% four days out, because its nuclear
phase-out makes the generation mix volatile and hard to forecast. A concrete driver
is nuclear maintenance. Belgian reactors take scheduled outages, and the phase-out
permanently retires capacity, both producing abrupt step changes in nuclear output
that no feature anticipates: the source network sees only past nuclear generation
and the calendar, with no outage schedule or available-capacity signal. In May 2026,
our Test~A month, Belgian nuclear sat at zero for a full outage the model cannot
foresee (confirmed from the Electricity Maps power-breakdown data used throughout
this study), which distorts the entire generation mix and the carbon intensity derived
from it. Feeding production this noisy into Tier~2 is why Belgium's two-tier model
is far worse than its single-tier one, and the damage starts at the shortest
horizon. Singapore is
the easy case, holding near 3 to 5\% throughout. PJM, NYIS, and Finland sit in
between and degrade steadily with horizon (PJM 4.8\% to 10.6\%), the regime where
CarbonCast's iterated 24-by-4 scheme could help, at the risk of compounding error
across blocks. Because the single-tier model uses no Tier~1 input and already wins,
improving Tier~1 is about rescuing the two-tier design rather than beating the
single-tier baseline.

% §9.2 Other limitations
\subsection{Other limitations}
\label{sec:other-limits}

\textbf{Test set involvement in window selection.} The per-zone training windows
(Finland and Belgium both 2024-onward; stable grids full history) were chosen by
observing accuracy on both Test~A and Test~B (Section~\ref{sec:findings}). Neither
test set is therefore a fully independent held-out evaluation for the window
hypothesis. The window result is reported as a diagnostic and design finding, not as
a validated generalization to unseen data.

\textbf{Weather optimism in the head-to-head.} Our Test~B models were replayed
on actual realized weather while Electricity Maps used a real forecast at issue
time. Because Electricity Maps already leads in all five zones under this optimistic
condition, a fair weather-matched replay would widen every gap further. The
magnitude of this systematic bias is unquantified. A faithful replay on the
captured weather forecasts is listed as a next step (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\textbf{Out-of-fold training on short windows.} Finland and Belgium train from
2024-onward, giving roughly one calendar year of data. The leave-one-year-out
out-of-fold scheme used to generate Tier~1 training forecasts for Tier~2 therefore
reduces to a single train/predict split rather than a true cross-validation, making
the out-of-fold forecasts less representative of Tier~1's inference-time error profile
than on the stable grids with five years of history.

\textbf{Flow ablation scope.} The cross-border flow ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:flowcompare}) was run on the two-tier consumption model only,
the one framework built to exploit those channels as Tier~1 forecasts. Whether
raw current-value flow features would help the single-tier model is not tested.

\textbf{Forecast horizon cap.} The Electricity Maps academic forecast horizon is
24 hours rather than the advertised 72, so the head-to-head (Test~B) is restricted
to the 1 to 24 hour band.

% ── §10. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We built an open, reproducible deep learning framework for short-horizon
consumption-based carbon intensity forecasting and evaluated it across five
structurally diverse grids. The framework is broadly consistent with the closed CarbonCast
method as a production-based baseline, with the PJM two-tier model landing at
6.0 MAPE against the published 5.3, and extends it to a consumption-based target
with explicit cross-border flow and partner carbon intensity inputs.

Two results stand out. First, the per-region atlas shows wide variation across
regions that is descriptively consistent with each grid's structural characteristics:
a tight high-carbon island grid (Singapore) is trivial to forecast, the large US
markets sit near 6\% MAPE, and the small import-heavy, rapidly decarbonizing
European grids are hardest, with Belgium the clear failure case driven by a May 2026
distribution shift. Both transitioning grids use a 2024 onward training window, motivated by their
documented regime shifts: recency helps a grid in rapid transition, while the
stable grids still do best on full history.
Second, the structural comparison overturns the assumption behind the two-tier
design: a single-tier network that predicts carbon intensity directly matches or
beats the two-tier model in every zone, decisively so for the consumption target.
The cause is visible in the first tier, whose generation forecasts are imperfect
enough that passing them downstream adds noise rather than signal. This does not
contradict CarbonCast, whose two-tier benefit is staying accurate when source
data is missing, measured against a fixed equation baseline, not against a learned
single-tier model.

Two further results complete the picture. Within the two-tier consumption framework, the flow on/off comparison shows the
cross-border inputs do not improve accuracy: the model is as good or better without
them in four of five zones, for the same reason the second tier fails, since the
flow channels are themselves noisy forecasts. And on a live June 2026 window, with models replayed on actual rather than forecast
weather, the open model is within two MAPE points of the closed Electricity Maps
operational forecast in three of five zones with larger gaps in the two transitioning grids.

The remaining next steps are narrower than the contributions themselves: a faithful
replay of the head-to-head on the captured weather forecasts rather than on actual
weather, a longer Test~B window, and a broader study of how far the per-zone training
window should shorten for grids in transition.

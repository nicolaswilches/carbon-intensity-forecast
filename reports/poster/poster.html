Open, Reproducible Consumption-Based Carbon Intensity
Forecasting with Explicit Cross-Border Flow Modeling
Nicolás Higuera Wilches
IE University · School of Science and Technology
MSc Data Science and Business Analytics · Capstone 2026
Advisor
Prof. Bissan Ghaddar
A simple model that predicts carbon intensity directly beats the standard two-tier pipeline in every grid tested, and lands within a few percentage points of the closed operational forecast.
1   Problem and Gap
  • Compute workloads can shift to cleaner grids and greener hours to cut emissions — but this requires accurate, local carbon intensity (CI) forecasts.
  • Production-based CI counts only local generation. Consumption-based CI also accounts for imported electricity — a truer picture for consumers.
  • Most open forecasters target production-based CI. The one operational system that targets consumption-based CI (Electricity Maps) is closed-source.
  • We open the problem: a reproducible deep learning framework, five structurally diverse grids, explicit flow modeling, benchmarked head-to-head against the closed system.
2   Approach
Five grids: PJM, NYIS, Finland, Belgium, Singapore. We compare four framework variants on two CI targets (production and consumption).
Two-tier: Tier 1 ANNs forecast generation and flows; Tier 2 CNN-LSTM turns those into CI. Orange boxes = our flow extension.    Single-tier: CNN-LSTM predicts CI directly from raw features, no intermediate step.
3   Data and Setup
  • Data: Electricity Maps (academic license) — hourly CI, power breakdown, flows. Open-Meteo weather (ERA5 history, GFS forecast). Jan 2021 to May 2026, five zones.
  • Split: train 2021-2025, validate Jan-Apr 2026, Test A May 2026 (full 1-96 h horizon).
  • Test B (live collection): Jun 8-21 2026 — Electricity Maps forecast captured as issued (non-recoverable), enabling head-to-head on the 1-24 h band.
  • RMSE loss, Adam with gradient clipping, seed-averaged results.

5   Finding 1 — Two-Tier Complexity Does Not Pay Off
Single-tier matches or beats two-tier in every zone, decisively on the consumption target. We also reproduce CarbonCast: production two-tier PJM 6.04% vs. published 5.29%.
MAPE by zone and framework, consumption-based CI. Orange = single-tier (ours). Blue = two-tier. Lower is better.
6   Finding 2 — Cross-Border Flows Do Not Help
Removing interconnector and partner-CI inputs leaves the model equal or better in four of five zones. Belgium is actively hurt (MAPE 29.0 without flows vs. 58.5 with flows).
Why: Tier 1 flow forecasts over a 96 h horizon are noisy — they add error, not signal. The diagnostic below shows error grows with horizon and peaks where two-tier fails most.
Tier 1 total-generation forecast error (normalized RMSE) by horizon, per zone. Belgium error reaches 18-25%, the highest in the study — matching its worst two-tier performance.

Our open model lands within a few percentage points of the closed operational forecast, and beats it in Finland.

Conclusions
  • An open, reproducible framework reaches near-operational accuracy on stable grids.
  • The two-tier decomposition and cross-border flow features do not improve a learned single-tier model — both add noise through imperfect Tier 1 forecasts.
  • Per-region heterogeneity is large and structurally explained. Belgium remains the open failure case: distribution shift and unmodeled nuclear outages require zone-specific mitigation.
